About Oil & Gas Watch

In less than a decade, fracking and new oil and gas drilling techniques have turned the United States into the largest producer of oil and gas in the world. This surge of oil and gas production has led to a massive buildout of processing plants and refineries that convert fracked oil and gas into plastics, petrochemicals, synthetic fertilizer, liquid fuels, and electricity. This boom has also led to an exponential increase in the pipeline networks and storage hubs needed to transport the oil and gas from production wells to export terminals and other sites.

Oil & Gas Watch is a free, public inventory that tracks this rapid expansion of oil, gas, and petrochemical infrastructure throughout the United States. It maps the largest new and expanding oil, gas, and petrochemical infrastructure projects across the country and monitors each project’s construction status so the public can stay informed about major oil and gas developments being proposed in their communities. We track key environmental approvals companies must obtain before starting construction or operations and provide access to thousands of government records and permits unearthed by our analysts. This information provides a glimpse into how new and expanding facilities in these pollution-heavy industries could impact the environment and public health. The information in Oil & Gas Watch comes from public sources like government records, news articles, and corporate filings and disclosures.

Oil & Gas Watch was created by the Environmental Integrity Project, a non-profit, non-partisan organization founded by former EPA attorneys to improve enforcement of environmental laws. The Environmental Integrity Project is based in Washington, D.C., and Austin, Texas. This website was built by The Commons, a non-profit organization that builds digital infrastructure to support the environmental movement. Oil & Gas Watch is supported by Bloomberg Philanthropies, the Tilia Fund, and other foundation supporters.

  2. The potential air emissions increases (or decreases) from each new or expanding facility, including greenhouse gases (measured as carbon dioxide equivalents, or CO2e), and health-harming “criteria” air pollutants like sulfur dioxide (which damages the lungs), nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds (both of which contribute to smog), microscopic soot or particulate matter (which can trigger asthma and heart attacks), and carbon monoxide (which can inhibit oxygen intake to the heart and brain).
  3. The status of key environmental permits or approvals that companies must obtain before starting construction or commencing operations. These permits limit the amount of pollution that facilities can release into the environment and nearby communities by setting enforceable emissions limits or requiring companies to install and operate pollution control technologies. These permits offer opportunities for public input during public comment periods. It’s crucial for community members to participate in the public comment process, especially after a permit application has been submitted or a draft permit has been issued, to ensure that they have a voice in permitting actions.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can this data be used?
expand_more
expand_less

The Oil & Gas Watch dataset has a variety of uses and can be of service to everyone from academic researchers to frontline activists. The data is especially helpful for informing community members and decision-makers about the potential impacts from new and proposed oil, gas, and petrochemical projects across the U.S. and in their own backyards. Community organizers and engaged citizens can use the data to identify multiple projects in their neighborhoods to better understand their cumulative pollution and environmental justice impacts.

The permitting information can be used to improve community and stakeholder comprehension and engagement in public environmental permitting processes, and to ensure that government agencies and companies are held accountable under federal and state environmental laws.

If you have a specific question related to the use of this data or you’ve used our data in your work or project, please let us know at ogwatch@environmentalintegrity.org.

What is the “potential to emit” for a project, and how is it different from total facility-wide emissions or “actual” emissions reported by a facility that’s already operating?
expand_more
expand_less

It is important to note that Oil & Gas Watch shows information about potential emission changes from new or expanded infrastructure. Potential emission changes may differ from actual or reported facility-wide emissions.

Potential emissions, also called “potential to emit” or “PTE,” are defined as the maximum emissions that would result from operating the facility at full capacity around the clock (24/7, 8,760 hours per year), taking into consideration any federally enforceable requirements that may apply to the source, including emission limits and/or controls imposed by a federally enforceable construction permit. It’s calculated separately for greenhouse gases and criteria air pollutants, like particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide, carbon monoxide, and volatile organic compounds. It can be calculated for an entire facility (facility-wide PTE) or for a specific expansion project (project PTE). The project PTE represents net emission changes and can be interpreted as the overall emission increase or decrease compared to the facility’s previous facility-wide PTE. For additional information, visit the U.S. EPA’s website about New Source Review permitting.

Actual emissions are the emissions produced by the facility based on actual operating times and conditions. They can be higher or lower than potential to emit.

What are your data sources?

Given the complexity of oil and gas regulations in the U.S., we have many different data sources. What is not tracked on Oil & Gas Watch?
expand_more
expand_less

We do not track:

  • Every single permit that a company must obtain to build a new plant, pipeline, or expand or modify an existing one. For example, companies must obtain local approvals from counties, local municipalities, or townships. States may also require additional approvals to ensure the preservation of historic sites.
  • Oil and gas wells, and many small facilities like tank batteries.
  • Produced water disposal wells.
  • Gathering pipelines.
  • Elected officials or historical election outcomes in project areas.
  • Every existing oil, gas, or petrochemical facility or pipeline in the United States. For additional information about existing facilities, visit:
    • U.S. Energy Information Administration’s Energy Atlas, which provides data and interactive maps of energy infrastructure and resources in the United States.
    • EPA’s Enforcement and Compliance History Online database for facility-level pollution data and compliance history.

Our team of researchers is actively working to add the following information to Oil & Gas Watch:

  • New and expanding natural gas power plants
  • Clean Water Act Permits
  • Clean Air Act Operating Permits
  • Pipeline routes
  • Facility footprints
  • Maps of interconnected and related projects
  • Carbon sequestration and/or use projects associated with oil, gas, or petrochemical buildout
  • Links to compliance information for newly built or expanded operating facilities, including reported emissions and wastewater discharges

Specifically, why are potential greenhouse gas emissions often not included in the data for a facility or project?
expand_more
expand_less

We’re sometimes asked why Oil & Gas Watch includes potential greenhouse gas emissions (carbon dioxide equivalents, or CO2e) for some projects but not others. The potential emission data in Oil & Gas Watch comes from federal air construction permits and applications. We may be missing greenhouse gas numbers for some projects because the company hasn’t yet applied for an air construction permit, or the project doesn’t require an air permit because it won’t directly emit air pollution (like a seawater desalination plant).

In some cases, it’s a little more complicated. Potential greenhouse gas emissions are relatively straightforward to find for new major sources or major modifications to existing sources. These major sources have the potential to emit or increase emissions over certain thresholds (visit this EPA website for the nitty gritty details), and have to apply for and obtain air construction permits before they can begin construction. They must disclose their potential greenhouse gas emissions as part of that permitting process.

It’s harder to find the potential greenhouse gas emissions from a smaller source, or one that was permitted incrementally as separate small projects over time. This is because of how new sources of greenhouse gases are permitted under the Clean Air Act. Essentially, greenhouse gas permit limits only apply to “major” sources of criteria air pollutants, like particulates, nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide, carbon monoxide, and volatile organic compounds. If a new facility or expansion project doesn’t have the potential to increase emissions of those pollutants over their major source thresholds, it won’t have to obtain a permit that would limit its greenhouse gas emissions even if it otherwise would be a large source of greenhouse gas emissions. There are many oil, gas, and petrochemical expansion projects that have the potential to emit a lot of greenhouse gases but are permitted as minor sources under the Clean Air Act. But because of permitting requirements, information about their potential greenhouse gas emissions isn’t publicly available.

What’s the difference between a facility and a project in Oil & Gas Watch?
expand_more
expand_less

This question is best answered with a few examples. Consider an existing petroleum refinery — this is a facility in Oil & Gas Watch. If the refinery is expanding and building a new ethylene cracker — this expansion is a project at the refinery. The new ethylene cracker will be part of the refinery, and we consider it part of the facility. It is important to note that a state or the Environmental Protection Agency may consider the refinery and its new ethylene cracker separate facilities for federal air permitting purposes.

Now consider a brand-new nitrogen fertilizer manufacturing plant. This plant is a facility. The initial construction of the plant is a project. Any subsequent expansions or modifications of this plant would also be projects.

In Oil & Gas Watch, we define projects so they are consistent with permit approvals and/or corporate project announcements. In most cases, Clean Air Act New Source Review permits apply to only one project, like the new ethylene cracker at a refinery, but sometimes they cover several projects or parts of different projects.

Some environmental approvals apply to an entire facility, such as a Clean Water Act NPDES wastewater discharge permit or a Clean Air Act operating permit. Expansion projects may or may not trigger changes to these facility-wide permits.

What is the difference between an “announced” project and one that is considered “pre-construction”?
expand_more
expand_less

Announced projects have been proposed but the company has not yet submitted a Clean Air Act (CAA) air construction permit application or Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) application. Pre-construction projects have been announced and the company has applied for a CAA air construction permit or FERC authorization, but construction has yet to commence.

What does “deadline to begin construction” mean?
expand_more
expand_less

Under the Clean Air Act, the largest pollution sources are required to commence construction within 18 months after receiving the necessary permitting approvals. A permittee can receive an extension on this 18-month construction deadline if they provide satisfactory evidence to the relevant permitting authority that an extension is justified. This regulatory process has been established to ensure that air quality considerations and emissions limits remain current. It also allows state agencies to reevaluate the best available control technology requirements and to update permitting conditions if technological advancements have been made.

What is an associated facility, project, or pipeline?
expand_more
expand_less

These are new or expanding facilities, projects, and pipelines that are related to each other. There are also new and expanding oil, gas, and petrochemical plants that depend on existing infrastructure.

The connections between new, expanding, and existing infrastructure are not always easy to understand or trace. Oil & Gas Watch aims to highlight the connections and interdependencies among the new buildouts so that communities and decision-makers can understand the full scope of what could be built when a new oil, gas, or petrochemical facility or project is announced in their town or jurisdiction.

For example, a new liquefied natural gas (LNG) export terminal needs pipelines to transport natural gas from where it is extracted from the ground to where it can be processed, stored, liquified, and shipped to other countries for use as fuel or feedstock. We call these inter-related pieces of infrastructure ‘associated projects’ and ‘associated pipelines’ in Oil & Gas Watch.

Pipeline routes

Adding pipeline routes to Oil & Gas Watch is an ongoing collaborative effort between Fractracker Alliance, Healthy Gulf, and the Environmental Integrity Project. These routes supplement the information and records on the Oil and Gas Watch database and help visualize the connectivity between various oil and gas infrastructure projects.

Researchers from Fractracker Alliance, Healthy Gulf, and the Environmental Integrity Project use GIS mapping software to convert routes from paper or PDF maps to geospatial data. In some cases, map files are available from databases maintained by state agencies. Many of the routes correspond with expansion projects — in these cases, only the new pipeline segments associated with the expansion project are displayed. Pipeline routes may change as the project progresses — refer to the date of the source document for determining the date of the displayed pipeline route. You can access the GIS data and associated metadata here. If you have any questions about the dataset, please email ogwatch@environmentalintegrity.org.

About our partners:

FracTracker Alliance maps, analyzes, and communicates the risks of oil, gas, and petrochemical development to advance just energy alternatives that protect public health, natural resources, and the climate. They support groups across the United States, addressing pressing extraction-related concerns with a lens toward health effects and exposure risks to communities impacted by the oil and gas industry.

Healthy Gulf collaborates with and serves communities who love the Gulf of Mexico by providing the research, communications, and coalition-building tools needed to reverse the long pattern of overexploitation of the Gulf’s natural resources. They use research and organizational expertise to identify and assess the threats that Gulf communities face and analyze the impacts on communities and natural resources to develop strategies for tackling those threats.

How accurate is the pipeline route data?
expand_more
expand_less

Route accuracy varies from pipeline to pipeline. The parameters used to categorize the resolution of our route data, described below, can be used as a guide for the general level of accuracy of the pipeline route. Pipeline routes may change as the project progresses — refer to the date of the source document for determining the date of the displayed pipeline route.

High resolution: Source document used for digitizing the pipeline route is at a fractional scale greater than 1:24,000.

Medium resolution: Source document used for digitizing the pipeline route is at a fractional scale between and including 1:48,000 - 1:24,000.

Low resolution: Source document used for digitizing the pipeline route is at a fractional scale less than 1:48,000.

Mixed resolution: Pipeline route contains multiple resolution types. Refer to the Notes field for more information.

Unknown: The resolution of the source document used for digitizing the pipeline route or the source of the pipeline route is unknown.

Why do some pipeline projects not have routes?
expand_more
expand_less

Our pipeline routes dataset is dynamic and changes over time as new routes are added to Oil and Gas Watch. If a specific pipeline project does not yet have a route uploaded, it’s likely we are working on compiling the route data. If there are no new pipeline segments being constructed as part of the pipeline project, then there will be no corresponding digitized route.
